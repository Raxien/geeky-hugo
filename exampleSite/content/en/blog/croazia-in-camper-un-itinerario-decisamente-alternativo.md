Having left [Slovenia](/blog/slovenia-campervan-5-days-mountains-villages/) behind, we decided to explore, on the road, a part of **Croatia often overlooked by tourism**, far from the splendid seaside resorts and postcard images of crystal-clear sea.

Croatia's inland landscape is characterised by vast plains growing wheat, corn and sugar beet, stretching between hills and areas of karst rock.

## 5 stops through an unknown Croatia

Our **5-stop itinerary**, in which we also share the places where we stayed overnight, allowed us to visit not only the capital, Zagreb (by the way, check out [our one-day tour of the city here](/blog/zagreb-in-one-day-croatia/)), but also **old villages, forests, castles and ethnographic and birdwatching reserves**, all the way to the border with Bosnia and Herzegovina.

### Samobor

**Samobor**, about an hour from the capital, is a small village of pastel-coloured houses crossed by a stream and surrounded by woods offering plenty of hikes. Famous is **the Samobor Carnival**, which harks back to old literary and musical traditions. The scene is dominated from above by the ruins of the **castle**, while in the village it's not uncommon to find stalls selling local products like honey, biscuits and wine.
We stayed overnight for free at the **{{<extLink "bus station" "https://maps.app.goo.gl/18qSbD2dA51ggjBt9">}}**, in a large car park with bins and free wifi ({{<extLink "find out more about WiFi4EU" "https://wifi4eu.ec.europa.eu/">}}).

### Zagreb
**Zagreb**, a city with all the charm of old Europe, is at the same time rich in museums and contemporary art galleries that make it a modern destination too. We visited it while the marathon was taking place, in a decidedly youthful and festive atmosphere.
Is one day enough? If you don't have big plans to visit the museums too, yes.
We parked for free on a Sunday, 30 minutes' walk from the city centre ({{<extLink "here" "https://maps.app.goo.gl/3SK2tGKcJLDmV2jK6">}}), and stayed overnight, again for free, {{<extLink "near the zoo" "https://maps.app.goo.gl/JQhGAp6aEgCr1FYC6">}}.

### Čigoč
The village of **Čigoč** is "famous" for the many **stork nests** found on the wooden houses typical of the village: the best time to visit is July/August. Čigoč sits in the middle of **Lonjsko Polje Nature Park**, a marshy expanse in the Posavina region, home to storks and many other birds as well as horses, piglets, cows and wild boar.
In Čigoč you can walk a free 5-km nature trail, or take on proper paid trails (for a negligible fee of around 3 euros).
The atmosphere in October was slightly eerie, so much so that it felt like crossing a ghost town populated only by chickens and stray dogs. Free overnight stays aren't allowed, but if you want there's a campsite in the village centre costing 30 euros a night. We carried on to the next village.

### Krapje
The nearby village of **Krapje** is a decidedly more picturesque destination, with a single main street lined with traditional wooden houses. {{<extLink "Here" "https://pp-lonjsko-polje.hr/">}} you'll find an information centre with a handy car park where we stayed overnight for free, offering public toilets, picnic tables and wifi (WIFI4EU). Nearby, a paid trail leads into the forest, where you're not allowed to continue further given the high number of wild animals.

### Jasenovac
In the village of **Jasenovac** we wanted to pay a commemorative visit to the *concentration camp* used from 1941 to 1945 during *World War II*, the site of the extermination of Serbs, Jews, Roma and political opponents, and sadly of many children. Nothing remains on site today except an old rail track, a train, a memorial and the Memorial Museum.

From here you can cross the border into Bosnia and Herzegovina. We had to continue on to the town of **Slavonski Brod** to pick up the **SIM card** that gives us {{<extLink "unlimited internet worldwide" "https://connectpls.com/vandipety/">}} (discount code: vandipety), and we crossed the border here to reach Banja Luka and continue our adventures heading east.
